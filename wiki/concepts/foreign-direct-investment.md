# Foreign Direct Investment

**One-liner:** Cross-border investment in productive assets (factories,
subsidiaries, M&A) where the investor seeks lasting management influence,
distinct from portfolio capital.

## What it is

In the Vietnam context covered by [[fdi-vietnam-fair-sustainable]], FDI is
the cumulative engine that has built the country's exports-led growth
model. Cumulative Korean investment is reported at ≈ US$100bn (Samsung,
LG, Lotte, SK) and Japanese at ≈ US$80bn (Honda, Toyota, Panasonic,
Canon). The bulk of FDI into Vietnam is *manufacturing*-oriented and
*economically* motivated rather than politically motivated.

The article re-positions FDI not as a quantity-to-maximise but as a
relationship to be **balanced**: as Vietnam's domestic capacity matures,
both sides should be reaching for "fair and sustainable" benefit. This
aligns with the broader literature reviewed in *Shifting Shores* (World
Bank, 2024) and Hayakawa et al. (2026): macro stability, human capital,
infrastructure, and predictable rules dominate long-run attractiveness.

## How it works

FDI in Vietnam flows through three principal channels: (i) greenfield
manufacturing investment in industrial parks (the Korean and Japanese
flagship plants), (ii) supplier networks attached to those anchors, and
(iii) services FDI (telecom, finance, retail) which the [[fdi-vietnam-
fair-sustainable]] op-ed argues remains under-opened.

The country's bargaining toolkit comprises [[bamboo-diplomacy]] (balanced
geopolitical posture), an FTA network of 17 agreements (EVFTA, UKVFTA,
CPTPP, RCEP, ASEAN-Canada and Vietnam-EFTA in negotiation), and
investment-incentive regimes. Selection toward higher-value-added segments
is the policy shift the op-ed advocates.

## Open debates

- Whether Vietnam's bargaining stance can shift to "selection" without
  deterring marginal but useful investors ([[fdi-vietnam-fair-sustainable]]
  argues yes).
- How effective the 17-FTA network is at *utilised* market access vs.
  nominal market access.
