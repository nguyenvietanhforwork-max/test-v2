# Friend-shoring

**One-liner:** Relocating supply chains toward politically- or
economically-aligned partner countries to raise resilience under
geopolitical stress; sibling strategies are near-shoring and reshoring.

## What it is

Coined in post-Covid policy discourse, friend-shoring captures the
restructuring of multinational investment networks toward "trusted"
jurisdictions. As discussed in [[fdi-vietnam-fair-sustainable]], the
phenomenon splits into:

- **Political friendliness** — alignment with the home-country bloc
- **Economic friendliness** — alignment with cost/competence requirements
- **Near-shoring** — relocation closer to consumer markets
- **Reshoring** — return to the home country itself

Hayakawa et al. (2026), cited by the same op-ed, find political
friendliness dominant across Factory America, Factory Asia, and Factory
Europe, but economic friendliness leads inside Asia — a structural
tailwind for Vietnam.

## How it works

Multinationals (MNEs) restructure their supplier and production networks
under three pressures: pandemic shock, geopolitical tension and tariff
regimes, and rising protectionism. Resilience metrics (single-source risk,
geographic concentration, time-to-recover) are now first-order.
Governments often co-fund these moves through subsidies, tax holidays, or
re-export schemes ([[fdi-vietnam-fair-sustainable]]).

The World Bank's *Shifting Shores* (2024) cautions that the gains are
unevenly distributed: countries with stable macro, good infrastructure and
predictable rules capture the bulk.

## Open debates

- Whether "friend-shoring" is durable or simply a re-labelling of cost
  arbitrage with geopolitical cover.
- How fast political-friend criteria can override the embedded economic
  rationale of existing networks.
