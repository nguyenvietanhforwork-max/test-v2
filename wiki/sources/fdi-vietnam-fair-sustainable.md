# FDI in Vietnam: Toward Fair and Sustainable Benefit

> Op-ed by economist Võ Đình Trí arguing that amid global supply-chain
> reshuffling, Vietnam should leverage its macro stability and "bamboo
> diplomacy" to negotiate FDI deals oriented toward fairer, more sustainable
> long-term outcomes for both sides.

## Summary

Published 13 May 2026 in *Tạp chí Kinh tế Việt Nam* (VnEconomy), this op-ed by
Dr. Võ Đình Trí (UEH, IPAG Paris, AVSE Global) re-frames Vietnam's FDI strategy
for an era of fractured global value chains. The author opens by noting that
pandemic shock, geopolitical tension, tariff escalation and rising
protectionism have triggered three relocation patterns among multinationals:
friend-shoring, near-shoring, and reshoring [§Xu hướng dịch chuyển FDI].
Citing Hayakawa et al. (2026) and the World Bank's *Shifting Shores* (2024),
he argues that political-friendliness drives relocation across Factory
America, Factory Asia and Factory Europe — but in Asia, economic-friendliness
matters more.

Vietnam, he argues, sits in a sweet spot. Cumulative Korean investment is
near US$100bn (Samsung, LG, Lotte, SK) and Japanese investment near US$80bn
(Honda, Toyota, Panasonic, Canon). The country's "bamboo diplomacy" —
balanced relations with all five UN Security Council permanent members and
15 comprehensive strategic partners — plus 17 active FTAs (EVFTA, UKVFTA,
CPTPP, RCEP) make it uniquely positioned [§Xu hướng dịch chuyển FDI].

But weaknesses remain: domestic supplier quality ranks 116/137 (WEF), road
freight handles ~80% of cargo, and Vietnam ranks 43/140 on the World Bank's
2023 Logistics Performance Index [§Điểm cân bằng FDI cho Việt Nam]. The
op-ed's thesis: Vietnam no longer needs to compete at any price; reform
priorities (infrastructure, energy, administrative simplification, supplier
upgrading) coincide with what FDI investors already want, so a fairer
balance of benefits is both feasible and overdue.

## Deep Dive

The piece is structured as a strategic re-positioning argument with two
explicit sections: (i) the global shifting of FDI flows, and (ii) what a new
"balance point" looks like for Vietnam.

**Global FDI relocation context.** The author frames the present moment as
post-Covid, post-geopolitical-shock, and post-tariff-war. Multinationals
(MNEs) are restructuring investment networks to raise resilience, often with
visible government backing. Three strategies dominate: friend-shoring (split
into political and economic friendliness); near-shoring (closer to consumer
markets); and reshoring (back to the home country). Hayakawa et al. (2026)
find political friendliness dominant across the three "Factories" of America,
Asia, and Europe — but within Asia, economic friendliness ranks higher,
which is a tailwind for Vietnam. The World Bank's *Shifting Shores* (2024)
warns that the gains will be unevenly distributed, and long-run FDI
attractiveness still depends on business climate, macro stability, human
capital, tax policy, and infrastructure.

**Why Vietnam attracts.** The capital flowing in comes mostly from Korea,
Japan, Taiwan, Singapore, and Greater China (incl. Hong Kong). The author
emphasises that investors are drawn by economics — macro stability, fast-
growing domestic market, port system, cost-competitive young labour, FDI
incentives — more than by politics. He highlights "bamboo diplomacy" as the
political enabler: Vietnam holds Comprehensive Strategic Partnerships with
all five UN Security Council permanent members (China, Russia, USA, France,
UK) and 15 such partners in total. The 17 FTA network (EVFTA, UKVFTA, CPTPP,
RCEP, plus ASEAN-Canada and Vietnam-EFTA in negotiation) gives preferential
access to over 50 markets [§Xu hướng dịch chuyển FDI].

**Weaknesses to fix.** Four are named explicitly. (1) Domestic supplier
quality is ranked 116/137 by the WEF — well below Thailand, Malaysia,
Indonesia. (2) Labour costs and job-hopping concern MNEs. (3) Logistics:
road accounts for ~80% of cargo, port and rail capacity is constrained, and
the country ranks 43/140 on World Bank LPI 2023 — behind Malaysia,
Thailand, Philippines. (4) Administrative burden: overlapping regulations,
slow procedures, high compliance risk [§Điểm cân bằng FDI cho Việt Nam].

**Toward a new balance point.** The author's analytical move is to argue
that the bargaining position has shifted. Vietnam remains FDI-dependent but
no longer must "compete at any cost"; for MNEs, exiting Vietnam would mean a
costly search for a substitute. Crucially, what investors are asking for
(infrastructure, energy, admin simplification, supplier upgrading) is
already on Vietnam's reform agenda — so the two sides' interests are
aligned. Vietnam should therefore move from a "win attraction" stance to a
"strategic selection" stance, prioritising long-term, mutually beneficial
projects and pulling domestic firms further into global value chains in
higher-value-added activities [§Điểm cân bằng FDI cho Việt Nam].

**Reading the op-ed in context.** This piece is published alongside the
Vietnam Connect Forum 2026 (Nhịp cầu Phát triển Việt Nam) and the 25th
Golden Dragon Awards, signalling that the framing is intended to inform
official policy posture. The companion VnEconomy piece "Chiến lược thu hút
FDI vào Việt Nam: Chuyển từ thu hút sang lựa chọn chiến lược" (same day)
reinforces the message: shift from attraction to selection.
