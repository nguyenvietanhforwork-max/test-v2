# VASEP Proposes Removing Barriers to Reach US$12bn Seafood Export Target in 2026

> Vietnam's seafood exports rose 15% YoY to US$3.7bn in Jan–Apr 2026, led by
> a 52.3% surge to China. VASEP urges fixes to Circulars 81/2025 and 74/2025,
> IUU rule adjustments, labour stabilisation, and credit support to hit the
> US$12bn full-year target.

## Summary

Reporting from VnEconomy (12 May 2026, byline Chu Khôi), the article
synthesises VASEP's policy submission to a Ho Chi Minh City conference on
agricultural and seafood export promotion. Headline numbers: April 2026
exports of US$947.8m bring Jan–Apr to US$3.7bn, +15% YoY. China leads at
26.7% share with +52.3% growth, Japan at 13.8% with +1.8%, and the US at
13% with -7.4% — the only major market in decline [§Trung Quốc là thị
trường tăng trưởng cao nhất].

VASEP Secretary-General Nguyễn Hoài Nam frames 8–10% sector growth (≈ US$12bn
for 2026) as achievable contingent on government action on three fronts:
institutional reform, raw-material supply, and administrative procedure
streamlining [§Trung Quốc]. The Association's concrete asks:

(1) Amend Circulars 81/2025 and 74/2025 to unblock wild-catch seafood
exports to the EU and US — push digitalisation, simplify dossiers, harmonise
provincial implementation, especially for small-scale fisheries [§Sớm tháo
gỡ vướng mắc]. (2) Revisit "whole-lot" definitions for imported seafood and
revise fishing-zone partitioning, since vessels >15m can have low engine
power and forcing offshore operation raises costs. (3) Stabilise processing
labour and prevent over-aggressive labour export from worker-short regions.
(4) Build a comprehensive raw-material development programme (aquaculture +
catch + import), with seed quality, disease control, and IUU traceability
infrastructure investment. (5) Amend Food Safety and Environmental
Protection Laws toward risk-based, post-hoc inspection. (6) Set up a sector
export-promotion fund and attract private capital into aquaculture
infrastructure. (7) Continue preferential credit lines and strengthen
economic diplomacy in the US, EU, China, Japan, Korea, Middle East. (8)
Government support against trade-defence cases and forced-labour scrutiny
at import destinations.

VASEP's medium-term aspiration is US$15–20bn annual seafood exports.

## Deep Dive

**Market structure.** The article paints a clear picture of demand
realignment. China has decisively re-emerged as Vietnam's largest seafood
market, taking 26.7% share with extreme YoY growth (+52.3%), well above all
other top-15 destinations. Japan holds steady at 13.8% with a small +1.8%
gain, while the US is the only top market shrinking (13% share, -7.4%
YoY). The implication for Vietnamese exporters is that diversification
risk has flipped: pivot capacity toward Asia is paying off, but US-tariff
or trade-defence exposure remains a drag [§Trung Quốc là thị trường tăng
trưởng cao nhất].

**The 12-billion case.** Nguyễn Hoài Nam argues the sector can grow 8–10%
in 2026, equivalent to crossing US$12bn for the year. This is not framed as
automatic — he conditions it on "timely support from Government, ministries
and provinces" via institutional reform, compliance-cost reduction, and
handling international trade barriers. The piece structurally echoes a
producer-association lobbying document: pressures are listed (raw-material
shortage, labour shortage, high production cost, disease, trade-defence
investigations, strict technical and import requirements) and then mapped
to specific asks [§Trung Quốc].

**Regulatory asks in detail.** The most concrete asks target two
ministry-level circulars (81/2025 and 74/2025) governing wild-catch exports.
The Association wants the Ministry of Agriculture and Environment to
digitalise the workflow, simplify dossiers, and unify implementation across
provinces, especially for small-scale fishery operators where the current
regime is poorly fitted. On IUU enforcement, VASEP asks for a revisited
definition of "whole lot" for imported seafood (to match commercial reality)
and a re-think of fishing-zone partitioning. The technical argument is that
many >15m vessels have low engine power and limited offshore capability;
forcing offshore operation raises costs and shrinks legal raw-material
supply for export processing [§Sớm tháo gỡ vướng mắc].

**Industrial-policy ask.** VASEP wants the government to formally
commission it to draft a comprehensive seafood raw-material development
programme — aquaculture, capture fishing, and imports — aiming at the
medium-term goal of US$15–20bn annual exports. Priorities listed:
broodstock and seed quality, disease control, lower production cost, port-
infrastructure investment, vessel monitoring, and traceability databases for
IUU compliance.

**Labour and environment.** A standout ask is for the government and
provinces to stabilise the seafood-processing labour pool by limiting
aggressive labour export from regions where processors are short-handed, and
to plan industries on the same locality to avoid local labour competition.
On environment, VASEP wants risk-managed, post-inspection regimes for the
Food Safety Law and Environmental Protection Law, plus flexibility for
industrial-park tenants to bypass central wastewater treatment if their own
plant meets standards with continuous monitoring.

**Finance and diplomacy.** Continued preferential credit lines for the
agri-forestry-fisheries cluster are requested. Economic diplomacy is asked
to focus on the US, EU, China, Japan, Korea, Middle East — overlapping
with where trade-defence and labour-rights scrutiny is most active.

**Industry commitment.** The article closes with VASEP's own commitment:
technology upgrading, compliance capability, value-added product
development, and market diversification, towards a more transparent,
responsible, sustainable industry.
