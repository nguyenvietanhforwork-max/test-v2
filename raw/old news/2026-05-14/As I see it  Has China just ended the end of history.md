You can perhaps judge the rise and decline of a society by the quality of its public intellectuals. In the last century, the United States had some genuinely great thinkers such as Walter Lippmann and Hannah Arendt who addressed a literate public while producing enduring works that can still be read today with great benefit.

Now you have people like Francis Fukuyama and Sam Harris who may be studied in the future more as a symptom of their society. A podcast between the two last month went viral because the end-of-history guy now acknowledges authoritarian China may be a viable political model after all while democratic America looks less and less attractive to others.

“I think that the Chinese have created a pretty impressive system. It is authoritarian. It’s quasi-market-based and they are very successful at marshalling new technology,” Fukuyama told Harris. “They’re capable of innovating a lot of things we thought they weren’t able to do. And conversely, democracy, especially American democracy, looks like it’s falling apart … if the Chinese keep their development machine going, it may turn out that they have a real alternative.”

Fukuyama has [gone from](https://www.scmp.com/opinion/world-opinion/article/3328325/how-fukuyama-goes-end-history-end-west?module=inline&pgtype=article) “the West is the best” to “the end of the West is nigh”, and now seemingly, “the East is red”. Perhaps he does deserve credit for daring to change his views, though it may be just his readiness to follow whatever is trending at the moment. From the fall of the Soviet Union to the rise of Donald Trump, tracking post-Cold War America’s triumph to its hegemonic decline must have been a tragic journey.

Harris is a neuroscientist who comments on everything, including defending Israel’s genocidal war in Gaza. He thinks it is a myth that the two sides **–** the Israelis and the Palestinians – in this conflict are “equally civilised, equally entitled to respect, and equally worth protecting”.

I should think that support for equal rights would be the least controversial position to take in this terrible conflict.

I am not sure people still take Fukuyama seriously, though he can be amusing in the way you might enjoy watching a one-time Hollywood star end up acting in B-movies.

It is common among Western pundits and politicians to overestimate the West and underestimate China. The young Fukuyama went more overboard than most by claiming everyone else had no choice but to follow the West.

In the podcast, he said people from poor and badly governed countries would have “chosen the United States without question for most of the last several decades”.

“These days I’m not sure that it’s such an attractive model for many people,” he added.

In a recent Foreign Affairs essay, Neil Thomas and Shengyu Wang, both of the Asia Society, address the same question: Does China offer “a rival operating system”? They point out the obvious: China critics have often “underestimated” [China’s Communist Party](https://www.scmp.com/news/china/politics/article/3132921/how-chinas-communist-party-structured?module=inline&pgtype=article).

“Countless scholars also insisted that innovation could not emerge under Leninism. Dislike of the regime and neglect of Chinese sources has often led to analysis that affirmed democratic values and overlooked Beijing’s agency.”

Well, those Western assumptions seem laughable now.

Chinese President Xi Jinping’s internal party reforms, they argue, try “to do in politics what China has already done in economics: build a rival operating system that defies Western expectations.”

Beijing doesn’t need Western-style elections or rule of law to stay effective. Rather internal discipline and self-correction, Xi believes, “can generate enough accountability, legitimacy, and success to sustain \[Communist Party\] rule”, the pair wrote.

“Even a partially successful attempt to discipline the party without liberalizing would challenge the long-standing assumption that authoritarian systems must either democratize or decay.”

Meanwhile, in a farewell piece, Der Spiegel’s former China correspondent Georg Fahrion acknowledges what every Chinese knows but which most Westerners deny.

“The \[Chinese\] system delivers what many of its citizens desire. It satisfies not only material but also emotional needs,” he wrote. “While I continue to perceive it as often unlovable, many Chinese feel a deep patriotism. To them, the People’s Republic – which often seems unbearable from the outside – appears at least equal, if not superior, to Western democracies. This is not a particularly pleasant realization because it challenges our image of ourselves.”

Well, we all live in our own bubble.
